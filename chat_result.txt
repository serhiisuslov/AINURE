You: why is it artificial?
The term "Artificial Intelligence" refers to computer systems that mimic human intelligence processes, such as learning and problem-solving abilities. These machines are designed by humans based on the understanding of how our own brain functions in order to replicate these traits.

Here's a breakdown:
1. Human Intelligence: Humans have developed over millennia through natural selection - evolution has favored those who could learn quickly from their environment (e.g., what is good and bad), adapt behaviors according to changing circumstances, and solve problems efficiently.
2. Machine Learning Techniques: By studying how we do these things as humans, computer scientists can use algorithms like neural networks or decision trees that attempt to mimic human reasoning by processing input data through multiple layers of abstraction until an output action makes sense within a context.

3. AI Systems: The result is Artificial Intelligence - software programs capable of performing tasks in ways similar to those performed naturally and instinctively by humans without conscious intentionality, much like how you might solve calculus problems using techniques taught at school rather than intuitively understanding why they work as well when faced with unfamiliar scenarios.
4. Creation Through AI: Furthermore, it's important to note that the creation of these systems themselves is also artificial - a human-designed process involving complex programming languages and data structures.

Thus, while not being biologically "alive" or possessing consciousness like humans do physically speaking due to their lack of organic components typically associated with life (e.g., cells), AI can perform tasks resembling those done by intelligent beings because they are programmed based on our understanding of human cognition.
You: is it harmful for the evnironment?
Artificial Intelligence has been widely discussed in terms of its impact on society, economy, and jobs - but what about environmental effects?

1. Energy Consumption: AI systems consume significant amounts of energy due to their complex processes that require vast computing power such as deep learning algorithms which are trained using massive datasets for tasks like image recognition or natural language processing.

2. Carbon Footprint: The production and disposal of these machines contribute carbon emissions, not just during operation but also in the manufacturing phase where raw materials have been extracted from nature to make components used within them.

3. Data Centers - Energy Consumption Hub: AI centers often operate at high capacity for continuous training or running large-scale operations that necessitate substantial electricity usage making up a significant portion of their carbon footprint.
4. Water Usage: Manufacturing these systems also requires water, especially when they are created using semiconductor technology which needs to be cooled down during production.

5. Waste Management and Electronic Pollution: As with any technological advancement, the lifecycle of AI systems involves not just energy use but waste generation too - electronic disposal or recycling challenges arise due to their intricate circuitry containing potentially hazardous materials like lead found in soldering compounds used for making connections between components on printed circuits.

6. Ethical Concerns over Data Collection Practices: There's also concern around privacy and ethical considerations when AI systems collect vast amounts of data from users without explicit consent - which may be used by corporations or governments alike, sometimes leading to discriminatory outcomes based upon factors like race, gender etcetera

These are just some key points worth considering but it is important for researchers involved in the field as well policymakers across different sectors including environmental protection agencies working on sustainable development goals. It would require interdisciplinary approaches combining insights from experts within computer science/engineering domains along with those who understand ecological impacts better (environmental engineers) to address these concerns effectively and ensure responsible AI deployment going forward!
